Hispanic Influence and Family Storytelling: Songs

I am Ana Portillo, a Latina poet. This is a collection of Hispanic art that has inspired me. To start, I will list the Spanish music from the 1970s that my dad has shown me since childhood.

In order to understand the themes of my pieces, it is necessary to understand the influece Hispanic media, whether that be songs or poems, have had on my poetry. It is also necessary to read my work through the lens of the immigrant experience combined with Hispanic culture, as well as themes of family.

1. "La Foto De Carnet" by Leonardo Favio

One of the first songs my dad ever translated to me. I originally laughed hearing that my older sister cried when she was young and heard the translated version, but I soon understood why, and cried myself. This song incorporates narrative poetry in a way I hadn't seen in other pop songs, by having the song start with spoken dialogue between two people. It inspired me to write works that make people feel emotional, and had me think on themes of seperation.

2. "Me Olvide de Vivir" by Julio Iglesias

This song came into my life in the form of a used vinyl record from a used bookstore, and my dad recognized the artist. Not knowing any of his songs except the stories my dad told of listening to records and cds of his, I bought it. The song's sentiment is apparant in the title, "I forgot to live." It really resonated with me, to make sure I was living in the moment and cherishing my family while they are here. This also was very on-theme for the type of music my dad liked, which often was existential, contemplatng time passing, grief, and longing. This was the fist song on that vinyl, and it remains my favorite vinyl since.

3. “Mi Querido, Mi Viejo, Mi Amigo” by Roberto Carlos

This song relates to what I was talking about similar themes in the above song, because this song explicitedly discusses grief, specifically of the singer's father growing older and passing away. So, I have always seen art being used to express these feelings of love and loss. I tend to also write about these subjects as well, and I often tie Spanish words into it. For example, I wrote a poem called "Viejito" which came from a discussion I had with my dad about this song, and how he called his dad this while he was still alive. I also use the endearing term to my dad, to surpass the language barrier that exists quietly in the background of our relationship. He is completely fluent in English, but I want to also surpass that gap to have my dad experience hearing Spanish from his daughter, even though I don't speak fluently.
